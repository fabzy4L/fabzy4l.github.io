Executive Summary

Biological medicine is experiencing its most consequential decade. Cell and gene therapies have delivered functional cures for previously untreatable conditions — sickle cell disease, certain blood cancers, rare genetic disorders. Yet the fundamental limiting constraint is not science. It is manufacturing.

The global CGT manufacturing market is valued at $11.6 billion in 2024 and is growing at 26.6% CAGR toward $123 billion by 2034. The underlying therapy market — which manufacturing serves — will reach $146 billion by 2035. The gap between clinical promise and patient access is not a regulatory gap or a science gap. It is an operational gap.

CAR-T cell therapy costs $120,000–$300,000 to manufacture per dose. Total cost of treatment, including hospital administration and logistics, can reach $2 million per patient. The therapies work. The factory doesn't.

LabOps.ai's thesis is simple and historically grounded: what Ford did to the automobile — making it manufacturable at scale for ordinary people — must be done to biological medicine. Not incrementally, through bolt-on automation sold to existing CDMOs. But fundamentally, through an AI-native operating system for biomanufacturing that owns the intelligence, the process, and eventually the infrastructure.

LabOps.ai is not a CDMO. It is not a software tool. It is not a bioreactor company. It is an intelligent infrastructure operator — building the manufacturing layer that the next hundred approved cell and gene therapies will need to actually reach patients, at prices patients can survive.

Background: The Biological Medicine Imperative

To understand LabOps.ai's market position, you must first understand the nature of what is being manufactured — and why it is categorically different from every pharmaceutical that came before it.

Pharma 1.0
Small Molecule Drugs

Chemical synthesis. Repeatable. Scalable. Shelf-stable for years. One batch serves millions. COGS pennies per dose.

Pharma 2.0
Biologics / mAbs

Protein manufacturing via cell culture. More complex. Cold chain required. Months of production. COGS hundreds of dollars. Requires CDMOs.

Pharma 3.0
Cell & Gene Therapies

Living drugs. Patient-specific. Manufactured in days to weeks per patient. Cannot be stockpiled. COGS $120K–$300K per dose. The factory IS the drug.

The Living Drug Problem

Cell and gene therapies are fundamentally different from every prior category of medicine. They are not manufactured from chemicals or proteins — they are manufactured from living cells. The manufacturing process is inseparable from the drug itself: the conditions under which T-cells are expanded, the timing of transduction, the quality of the viral vector, the temperature of every step — these are not quality checkpoints. They are the product.

As of February 2025, the FDA has approved seven CAR-T cell therapies. There are over 6,000 active interventional cell therapy trials globally, with more than 2,154 involving genetically modified cells. Over 300 cell therapies are currently in clinical development. The approval pipeline is accelerating — nine new CGT products received approval in 2024 alone.

The science has outrun the factory. This is the founding condition of LabOps.ai's market.

Market Segmentation

The CGT market is not one market — it is a stack. The therapy developers sit at the top. Below them is the manufacturing layer: CDMOs, in-house facilities, and emerging IDMOs. Below that is the tooling and reagents layer: bioreactors, vectors, media, quality systems. LabOps.ai targets the manufacturing and infrastructure layer — the highest-value, least-solved, most capital-intensive segment in the stack.

The CGT manufacturing market specifically — the segment LabOps.ai competes in — was $11.6 billion in 2024 and projects to $123 billion by 2034 at 26.6% CAGR. The CDMO segment within that is $8.2 billion in 2025, growing at 27.9% CAGR to $75 billion by 2034. North America currently holds 48% of manufacturing market share. Asia-Pacific is the fastest-growing region.

68% of current manufacturing is contract-based — meaning most therapy developers do not own their manufacturing. This creates enormous leverage for the entity that controls the intelligent manufacturing layer.

Critical Market Dynamics

Allogeneic Is Rising

Autologous (patient-specific) therapies dominate at 59% market share today, but allogeneic (off-the-shelf) approaches are growing fastest. Allogeneic manufacturing is fundamentally more scalable — one batch serves many patients. This structural shift favors intelligent large-scale operators.

Decentralization Is Inevitable

Point-of-care manufacturing — where therapy is produced at or near the clinical site — is emerging as the dominant future model. The UK's MHRA implemented PoC manufacturing licenses in January 2025. FDA issued multisite CAR-T guidance in 2025. The regulatory framework is catching up to the technology.

AI Is the Differentiator

Real-time process control, predictive quality assurance, and AI-enabled analytics are becoming table stakes for competitive manufacturing. OmniaBio targets 50% cost reduction via AI/robotics. The companies that own proprietary process intelligence — not just hardware — will dominate.

The Financial Reality

Unit Economics: The Core Problem

The economics of cell therapy manufacturing are broken at first principles. A single autologous CAR-T treatment costs $120,000–$300,000 to manufacture. By the time hospital fees, quality management, transportation, and administration are included, total cost of care can reach $2 million per patient.

This is not a pricing anomaly. It reflects the true cost of bespoke, manual, expert-dependent biological manufacturing. Gilead has publicly stated its goal of achieving ~80% gross margins (biologics-like) for its CAR-T business by 2030 — which requires reducing Breyanzi's manufacturing COGS from an estimated $200K–$360K per dose in 2023 to approximately $80K by 2030.

That trajectory — cutting COGS by 75–80% over seven years through manufacturing improvement — is the market LabOps.ai is designed to accelerate and capture.

Investment Flow

Capital is moving into this space at scale. AI for biopharma attracted over $1.6 billion in the first half of 2025 alone. Investment in AI for biopharmaceuticals increased by nearly $2 billion year-over-year from 2021. Corporate AI investment across all sectors hit $252 billion in 2024, with private investment rising 44.5%.

Current Infrastructure State

Understanding the infrastructure gap is understanding LabOps.ai's opportunity. The manufacturing landscape is fragmented, manual, and structurally incapable of meeting the volume demands of an approved pipeline that is doubling every two years.

⚗️
COGS Crisis

CAR-T manufacturing runs $120K–$300K per dose today. Total treatment cost reaches $2M/patient when hospital fees and logistics are included. Even with projected improvements, sub-$100K COGS by 2030 is an aspiration, not a guarantee.

🧑‍🔬
Talent Scarcity

Cell and gene therapy manufacturing is artisanal — dependent on a small pool of highly trained scientists. Manual processes introduce contamination risk and batch variability. The workforce cannot scale at the rate the pipeline demands.

🏭
Infrastructure Deficit

Most manufacturers use disconnected, semi-automated, single-step solutions. Fully integrated closed systems are just beginning to emerge. Modular GMP suites are expensive and slow to deploy. 74% of current manufacturing is still pre-commercial R&D scale.

🚚
Vein-to-Vein Latency

Autologous therapies require patient cells to be harvested, shipped to a centralized facility, manufactured, QC'd, and shipped back. This vein-to-vein timeline stretches weeks to months — clinically dangerous for aggressive cancers.

🌍
Access Asymmetry

Manufacturing is concentrated in North America and Europe. Patients in Asia-Pacific, Latin America, and Africa — representing billions of people — have almost no access to approved therapies that cost millions per dose.

📋
Regulatory Complexity

Each facility requires site-specific validation of sterility, potency, identity, and stability. Decentralized manufacturing at scale requires novel regulatory frameworks that are only beginning to emerge globally (UK MHRA PoC license, FDA multisite CAR-T guidance 2025).

The CDMO Paradox

CDMOs are the current answer to the manufacturing gap. 68% of all CGT manufacturing is outsourced. The CGT CDMO market is growing at 27.9% CAGR. Samsung Biologics announced a $4.8 billion investment for a new CGT bio-campus in December 2025.

But CDMOs are structurally limited. They are service businesses — they manufacture someone else's drug using someone else's process. Their incentive is billable hours and capacity utilization, not process innovation. Their margins are compressed by client competition and capital intensity.

Most critically: CDMOs optimize for existing processes. LabOps.ai is building for the process that doesn't exist yet — one where intelligence drives the manufacturing decisions, not protocol binders.

The Automation Gap

As of 2024, most CGT manufacturers are still operating disconnected single-step or semi-automated solutions. 74% of manufacturing is still pre-commercial R&D scale. The industry is just beginning to migrate to fully automated, closed-system platforms.

The transition from manual to automated is well underway at the hardware layer — Ori Biotech's IRO platform, Cellares' Cell Shuttle, Cellino's Nebula system are all solving pieces of this. But none of them address the intelligence layer: the continuous learning system that monitors, predicts, adapts, and improves across every run, every product, every facility.

This is the LabOps.ai layer. Not the bioreactor. The brain.

The Ford Analogy — Unpacked

Before Ford, automobiles were hand-built by craftsmen. Each car was bespoke. The cost was prohibitive. Ownership was for the wealthy. The factory was organized around skill, not systems.

Ford's insight was not technical — it was operational. He systemized the production process, standardized components, created continuous flow, and eliminated the craftsman as a variable. The Model T was not a better car than what came before it. It was a more manufacturable one. And because it was manufacturable, it was accessible.

Cell and gene therapy today is pre-Ford. It is artisanal. Expert-dependent. Bespoke. LabOps.ai is the Ford moment — but instead of flow lines, the system is AI-orchestrated bioprocessing, robotic handling, and modular infrastructure that can be standardized, replicated, and continuously improved at network scale.
